THE DECENTRALIZED INTERNET

For people who watch the popular HBO series Silicon Valley which is based on startups that promise to "change the world" by tackling simple and non-existent problems. In their latest pivot, Richard Hendricks and the Pied Piper gang are trying to create a new internet that cuts out intermediaries like Facebook, Google, and the fictional Hooli. Their idea: Use a peer-to-peer network built atop every smartphone on the planet, effectively rendering huge data centers full of servers unnecessary.

So, what is the decentralized internet actually?

Let me explain that, the internet runs on huge cloud servers or data centers which are centralized and owned by major corporations like Google, Facebook, Amazon, etc. sure anyone can actually post content on the internet but without Facebook and Google, will anyone be able to find it? Amazon, meanwhile, controls not just the web's biggest online store but a cloud computing service so large and important that when part of it went offline briefly in 2017, it seemed as if the internet went down. The decentralized internet is the next big paradigm where the data is secure it is quoted as being a kind of “Digital Garden of Eden” where the data is controlled by the user. Suppose I were to type “red sneakers” in my google search bar, this data is sent to ad servers which are then sold to companies that send in advertisement pop-ups of red sneakers on the next pages I visit. one may argue that Google’s privacy policy states that our data is in secure hands but we have to leave it on blind faith that google will keep its promise which by the way it is not.

How Will We Achieve Ideals of a Decentralized Internet?

A Peer to Peer Network:

This is the idea showcased in the series Silicon Valley which I previously talked about their idea is to use a peer-to-peer network built atop every smartphone on the planet, effectively rendering huge data centers full of servers unnecessary. On the show, Richard's plan stems from the realization that just about everyone carries around a smartphone with hundreds of times more computing power than the machines that sent humans to the moon. What's more, those phones are just sitting in people's pockets doing nothing for most of the day. In exchange for using their app, users would agree to share some of the extra space they free up using Pied Piper's fictional compression technology, who will then resell it to companies for far less than they currently pay giants like Amazon. Suppose we want to create a decentralized internet or maybe a part of it here in VBIT what do we do? we need a miniaturized data center to run a small version of the internet here in VBIT implementing this we could actually accumulate some part of the memory from every student i.e. memory from their smartphone which is a lump sum would be a lot of memory which could eventually be bigger than cloud server and voila! You have your own peer to peer network, now data can be stored, shared and accessed over this network which is completely isolated from the centralized internet. Imagine if we were to implement this on a global scale, we would no longer require the centralized internet. The data stored will be encrypted which is stored in small fragments over this network of smartphones hence data can be accessed and shared over this wonderful secure system.

Decentralized Apps or D Apps:

well I typed this article over Google Docs, the online word editor from Google where all the information is sent to their cloud servers for every change compromising on my privacy. Despite Google's privacy policies and strong reputation for security, it has the technical ability to do whatever it wants with information I entrust to it to overcome this there is this Application which is similar to Google docs called Graphite Docs only difference it is secure.. Graphite Docs is one of those many apps built upon the ideology of a decentralized and secure internet dubbed as D Apps. When I typed the above sentences in Graphite Docs, noting Google’s shortcomings it automatically accorded it a higher level of protection. I could still access and edit my document from different computers, because it was backed up online as I worked. But the data was stored in an encrypted form, on a network of computers unable to read my data. The encryption keys needed to unscramble it never left my own devices, meaning that unlike most of the online services we use, my data was solely under my control. All that is possible because Graphite Docs is built on top of Block stack, a platform for decentralized internet apps developed by a startup of the same name. You access apps on the platform through a browser, but they run locally, on your computer, with help from software you install from Block stack. That software helps you create the ID you need to log in to Block stack apps and store your encryption keys. And it gives you a choice of where you want to store your encrypted data: your own server or the Gaia storage network powered by Block stack and some early adopters who have contributed their own computers to the cause. You can access your data from anywhere—as long as you remember your 12-word encryption key phrase. Many decentralized internet projects like Graphite Docs, are pitched as more private versions of existing products. Example D Tube the decentralized you tube although the videos are slow to load there weren't any ad’s and neither did ad agencies haunt me with baking products since I watched a video on how to bake a cake.

To conclude, decentralization of the internet will be among the core ideologies of Web 3.0: The next big evolution of the internet which we are at the verge of if we are finally able to achieve the Utopian ideal of a decentralized internet it will be like a homecoming to the Digital garden of Eden where the data is under our control just like how it was at the start of the internet, “The Good Old Days I say…..
